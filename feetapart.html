Making people fitter with Feetapart
Feetapart
Aug 2019 - Nov 2019
I worked as the Lead Product Designer with Feetapart(on a contract basis) to redesign some of the key features of their app. I managed a team of 2 graphic designers, a UX researcher and a motion designer. I was responsible for the overall UI/UX design changes on the app.
About Feetapart
How Feetapart works
Feetapart is a gamification based health and wellness platform that offers exciting rewards to you for walking steps. For every step you walk, the pedometer turns your steps walked into a currency called Feetcoins, which can then be spent to redeem exciting rewards.
The Problem
Feetapart was struggling with their products redemption rates with their existing store. They had some worrying data about user's engagement with their store, some of the users also told them they did not even know that they can redeem a product.
Target Users
  • Age:
    In the range of 16-60 years. Most users fall into the 18-36 range.
  • Background:
    The core users of the app are working professionals who are already fitness freaks or are looking to improve their fitness levels. A small part of the target users
  • Goals:
    The primary goals of our target users were: To improve their overall fitness, to track their daily fitness metrics and to redeem rewards with the feetcoins they had earned.
INTERVIEWING USERS
Gathering User Insights
While the founders of Feetapart had already gathered some quantitative insights, we conducted a user research session to gather actionable qualitative insights to implement on. We gathered the following set of insights from our interviews with the users.
We interviewed 10 participants in the age group of 19-42 in Delhi and Bangalore. 7 of the participants were already fitness conscious while 3 of the participants chosen were looking to improve their daily fitness. Some of the findings of our interviews were:
  • Lack of trust:
    The users did not trust the gamification apps. They worried these apps were not genuine.
  • Too much work is needed to redeem rewards:
    Users did not realise that they could start getting rewards as soon as they would earn a few feetcoins on Feetapart. They did not need to walk for hours before redeeming rewards.
  • Looking for instant gratification
    The majority of our target users were millenials, they believe in instant gratification.
Fixing app trust issues
Our assumption for the lack of users trust in the app was because the visual design of the app was not very professional. Users did not There were a few generic visual design issues throughout the app, the icons throughout the app were not cohesive, had different stroke widths and stroke radiuses throughout the app. We started by building a cohesive icons system with well defined characteristics to be followed for future icons. In the first iteration, we replaced the existing icons throughout the app with our new icons.
We found that the navigation bar was ambigious, especially for the newer users, hence we added corresponding text labels to the icons in the navigation bar.
Iteration 1 and iteration 2 for the navigation bar
Testing the store with users
For gathering further insights on the store of the app, we gave a set of 5 users a task to be performed. The users would have to walk for a while to earn some feetcoins and then go to the app store and redeem rewards that they find exciting(or relevant to them).
  • The rewards that were shown to the users were not relevant to them, the rewards were shown in a simple list and appeared in the same order for every user.
  • Users did not realise that they had enough coins to redeem rewards.
Redesigning the Store
The Feetapart Store was a key feature for the app, since that was the part of the app that rewarded users for the fitness work they did on the app. It was the most important part of the hook, but in the current state, it wasn't converting.
Brainstorming and sketching out options
Along with the founders, I sketched out ideas for the redesign for a couple of days, looking at some of the obvious solutions to the problems we identified earlier. Once we discussed the sketches and settled on a couple of directions, I moved to creating mockups for the store.

Designing the Feetapart Store
Mocking up the approaches
We finalised on a couple of directions we can work further on, I mocked up both the approaches to have further discussion on. One of the approaches was to use a tabular structure with the available rewards and the redeemed rewards being the two tabs. The another approach was to use chips to show the most relevant products to the user straight away and allow a quick filter with chips to browse through the products that were close to them, unlocked for them etc. This is the approach we felt solved our problems in an elegent way.
Early Approaches
Designing the Feetapart Store
Settling on the Chips approach
We built on the Chips approach with further testing this users, we found that the gender of the user, how far away the product was from the user(location) and what products they could already redeem were the factors that made a product desirable for the users, hence we showed these filters as the easiest filters to be applied.

Designing the Feetapart Store
Iteration 1(and learning by testing)
We mocked up the chips approach, created a prototype and began testing this prototype with our target users. In our approach, we had decided to hide the feetcoins balance on the store screen to make sure that the scrolling of products on the screen was uninterruptive and smooth. Another assumption we made was that the low feetcoins balance could demotivate users. But while testing, we realised that it was too complicated for the users to calculate the feetcoins

Making Solutions scalable
Designing for scalability
We tried to keep scalability of the solution in mind right from the start and made sure what we create eventually would be scalable for considerable amount of time in future. The products lists, the featured products and the categories section has been designed to make sure that this can be scaled in future with little changes needed.
Designing the Feetapart Store
Measuring Impact and Further Improvements
Feetapart launched the new store on 1st January, 2020 and based on the analytics we recorded for the first week as well as the messages received from power users, it was an instant success. The products redemption rate grew by ~900%, more than 16% of the total users checked a product out on the store. The change was also reflected in the rating scores users gave to the Feetapart app on the Google Play Store. The rating improved from a score of 3.3 to 4.2 within the first 3 weeks of launch. As a result of this impact, the Feetapart app trended on #3 in the Fitness Category on the Play Store for a week.

FEETAPART STORE ANIMATIONS
Using animations to create a unified experience
The animations used for the store are designed to be short, friendly and non jerky. The animations help outline the importance and heirarchy of elements of the store. The "New Arrivals" category is the stand out element on the store and hence is animated with the slowest timing function, so that the attention of the user is diverted to it.
